Behavioral Framework:

Social Behavioural Framework

Federico Motta, 2025

Executive Summary
Digital engagement is often interpreted through shallow metrics: reach, impressions, likes. But what underlies those numbers is psychological. This framework proposes that meaningful digital engagement emerges from the satisfaction of three core psychological rewards:

A. Relatability: the need to feel emotionally recognized
B. Self : the need to feel empowered and capable of growth
C. Social: the need to feel visible, appreciated, and connected

These rewards are rooted in two foundational psychological theories: Social Identity Theory and Social Learning Theory. Understanding these theories helps us move beyond performance metrics and into the realm of identity, observation, and motivation. We propose that every piece of digital content or experience should deliver at least one of these three rewards. We also define clear behavioral metrics that allow teams to measure how well each reward is activated in user behavior.

Theoretical Foundations

Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)

Social Identity Theory (SIT) emerged in response to minimal group experiments that showed how even arbitrary group distinctions could produce in-group favoritism. The theory fundamentally restructured how psychology understands self-concept as one deeply embedded in group affiliation. It posits that a person’s sense of self is composed of personal identity (traits, values, and experiences that distinguish the individual) and social identity (the part of one’s self-concept derived from perceived membership in social groups). SIT is underpinned by three interrelated cognitive processes: (1) Categorization: People simplify their social environment by categorizing themselves and others into groups. This reduces complexity but introduces stereotyping. Importantly, categorization lays the groundwork for bias and preference. (2) Identification: Once a group identity is adopted, individuals conform to its norms, behaviors, and symbols. Their self-worth becomes bound to the group’s status and coherence. This leads to internalization of group values, shaping not only what one believes, but what one perceives as true or good. (3) Comparison: People elevate their own in-group by comparing it favorably to out-groups. This produces in-group favoritism, which is tied to self-esteem. If the group is praised or performs well, the individual feels affirmed. If the group is criticized, the individual’s self-image can suffer. Research following Tajfel’s initial studies confirmed that even trivial group assignments trigger this bias as individuals strive for a positive social identity (Tajfel & Turner, 1979). In other words, belonging to a group becomes a source of motivation: people favor their in-group and its symbols because doing so reinforces their self-worth. SIT also suggests that individuals are motivated to achieve and maintain a positive self-image through their group memberships, a premise supported by extensive evidence linking group affiliation to self-esteem and well-being (Abrams & Hogg, 1988). Subsequent developments, like Self-Categorization Theory, further showed how people not only identify with groups but also take on group-based norms and look to prototypical group members for guidance (Turner et al., 1987).

Social Learning Theory (Bandura, 1977)

Bandura’s Social Learning Theory (SLT) transformed behaviorism by showing that learning doesn’t require direct experience. Instead, humans learn efficiently by observing others through a process called vicarious learning. This concept expanded the domain of reinforcement by inferring that outcomes experienced by others can alter a person’s future behavior. Bandura famously demonstrated through the Bobo doll experiments that children could imitate aggressive behavior just by watching a model, even without any direct reward, illustrating observational learning in action (Bandura, 1977). SLT identifies four critical components in this learning process: (1) Attention: Not all models are equally influential. For a person to learn, the behavior must capture attention. Salience is driven by novelty, emotional weight, similarity to self, or social status of the model. (2) Retention: Observed behaviors must be stored as mental representations (e.g., schemas or scripts) that can later be retrieved and applied in new contexts. Without this step, observation remains fleeting and does not change future behavior. (3) Reproduction: The observer must believe they can reproduce the observed behavior. This is where self-efficacy (one’s belief in their capacity to execute the behavior) plays a central role. If the model seems too different or the behavior too complex, the observer will disengage. (4) Motivation: Finally, the learner must have a reason to imitate the behavior. They need to perceive that enacting the behavior will lead to a desirable outcome. Motivation can come from direct reinforcement (receiving a reward for the behavior), vicarious reinforcement (seeing someone else be rewarded for it), or intrinsic satisfaction (internal gratification for doing the behavior). Importantly, SLT repositions humans not as passive recipients of environmental stimuli, but as symbolic processors: we observe, infer, evaluate, and selectively imitate behavior. An observer is more likely to model a behavior if the model is someone they identify with or if the behavior is seen yielding positive outcomes (Bandura, 1986). This underscores the concept of vicarious reinforcement: seeing another person rewarded for an action increases the learner’s motivation to adopt that action themselves (Bandura, 1977). SLT also emphasizes that people don’t mimic blindly, they think about what they see and the consequences that follow. In essence, we become what we see rewarded, especially when the model reflects our identity and the reward is meaningful.

Translating Social Psychology into Online Interaction

While SIT and SLT were originally formulated in offline environments, their psychological mechanisms apply with equal, if not greater, force in digital settings. Digital platforms simply magnify what these theories describe: the drive to belong, and the tendency to imitate what we observe. Two specific transitions are especially relevant to understanding online engagement through the Social Behavioural Framework:

1. Identity Recognition in Digital Environments
Social Identity Theory helps explain why users gravitate toward certain content or communities online. What they choose often reflects their desire to affirm their place in a perceived social group. Digital environments allow for rapid identity signaling through preferences, language, aesthetics, or affiliations. The process of social categorization happens algorithmically (e.g., content clusters, affinity groups), but the emotional effect remains human: users seek to see themselves reflected in what they consume. When this happens they experience identity affirmation.

2. Observational Learning at Scale
Social Learning Theory plays out in two key ways in digital ecosystems. First of all, users constantly observe others demonstrating knowledge, insight, or personal improvement. When these models are perceived as credible or similar to us, users extract actionable lessons. Secondly, users observe which types of expressions get praised, which identities are supported, which behaviors earn replies or likes. This shapes what users consider “acceptable,” “admirable,” or “valued” in that social space. In both cases, digital platforms serve as dense learning environments where users continuously update their behavior not just based on what content says, but on how others react to it.

The Social Behavioural Rewards

Reward I - Relatability

“That’s me. And that means I belong.”
Relatability Reward is the foundational emotional mechanism in the framework. It arises when a user encounters something that mirrors their internal experience: a thought they’ve had, a feeling they haven’t named, a behavior they assumed was private. That moment of mirroring fulfills a deep psychological function such as identity affirmation through recognition. Drawing from SIT, this form of engagement satisfies the need to maintain a coherent and positively regarded self-image within a social group. When people see content that reflects their own emotional or cognitive states, they implicitly experience in-group affirmation, confirming that their internal world is legitimate, shared, and socially situated. This is especially powerful when the group being affirmed is not officially named (e.g., “people who can’t sleep from overthinking”) but emotionally implied. In SIT terms, even an informal collective of “people who get it” can serve as an in-group that validates one’s identity. From an SLT perspective, relatability functions as emotional modeling. When a user sees someone express what they’ve felt but haven’t articulated, it sets a precedent: “You’re allowed to feel this way. Others feel this too.” This vicarious validation reduces internal dissonance and increases expressive permission. Seeing others reveal a similar experience effectively models that it’s socially acceptable to have that experience (Bandura, 1977). For the individual, seeing their private feelings reflected in a public space offers reassurance that their experiences are normal, thereby reducing feelings of isolation. Empirical research indicates that knowing a friend or peer is going through the same emotional situation can make people feel better than handling it alone (Wagner et al., 2015). In one study, simply believing that a friend was simultaneously sharing the same emotional experience led participants to report less negativity and even activated neural reward circuits associated with social connection (Wagner et al., 2015). In line with the fundamental need to belong (Baumeister & Leary, 1995), content that validates a user’s inner experiences provides a sense of social connection and acceptance. It tacitly tells the user, “your feelings make sense in a social context,” which powerfully affirms that they are not alone. In practice, this reward is often pre-cognitive as users don’t always realize they’ve “seen themselves” in content, but their behavior reflects it.

Behavioral Signature
The behavioral correlates of Relatability are clear. Likes indicate private resonance. A low-friction signal that the user emotionally or cognitively relates. It’s a quiet form of identity affirmation: “This is me.” Shares, however, carry a higher social intention. They often function not simply to say “this is me”, but to say “this is us.” Users share content not only to self-signal but to strengthen group coherence, especially when it reflects a relationship, mutual quirk, or shared frustration. In other words, the content becomes more meaningful when someone else understands it with you.

How to calculate Relatability
𝑅𝑒𝑙𝑎𝑡𝑎𝑏𝑖𝑙𝑖𝑡𝑦 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑒 = (𝐿𝑖𝑘𝑒𝑠 + 𝑆ℎ𝑎𝑟𝑒𝑠) / (𝑅𝑒𝑎𝑐ℎ × 100)
This metric captures the breadth of recognition: among all who saw this content, how many felt mirrored enough to quietly affirm it or share it as part of their identity?

Relatability in Topics and Formats
Every piece of content is composed of two fundamental layers: topic and format. While the emotional resonance of relatability emerges from recognition, what is being recognized (the topic) and how it is expressed (the format) are distinct yet interrelated mechanisms. Both can be deeply relatable, but for different psychological reasons. Topics reflect recurring emotional, cognitive, or social patterns. In classical rhetoric they’re referred to as topoi (τόπος), from the Greek for "places." These are thematic anchors that remain relevant across time because they mirror fundamental aspects of human life: heartbreak, social anxiety, imposter syndrome. These topoi endure because they tap into universal identity experiences, or, in our terms, relatable experiences. Formats, on the other hand, reflect structure, tone, length, visual layout, rhythm, linguistic style, or medium. A lo-fi meme, a movie, a 40 pages essay, or a play, each is a format. Formats are culturally constructed and temporally sensitive. What feels emotionally resonant on Instagram may not feel appropriate in a theater because the format violates the local group code, although the topic, and the relatable feeling associated with it, might be exactly the same. Think of a meme that says: “POV you just got rejected by your crush for the 10th time”. It expresses a highly relatable, timeless, intimate yet universal topic, which can be explored across the centuries, from Ovid’s Metamorphoses through Dante’s Comedy. From a SIT perspective, we can see formats as a form of group signaling. A user recognizes themselves in how it's said because that mirrors the communicative habits of a group they identify with. Formats often carry a semiotic charge: they communicate who this is for and who belongs in the interpretive community. From SLT, observing the format teaches the appropriate way to express similar ideas within their in-group. They learn what type of voice is rewarded, what kind of syntax is admired, and what structural choices get responses. Thus, while topics provide thematic continuity, formats demand cultural adaptability. To maintain high relatability over time, we must preserve the timeless topoi while continuously researching and adopting the most current expressive formats that resonate with specific platform-based or identity-based groups. Without this dual awareness, content risks one of two failures: being emotionally shallow (when it relies on trendy formats but lacks depth) or structurally outdated (when it clings to meaningful topics but ignores evolving expressive norms).

Example
"It's 2 a.m. and I just remembered something embarrassing I did 5 years ago."
Why it works: According to SIT, when users see this, it affirms they are not alone in their mental patterns; it normalizes and de-stigmatizes their internal dialogue. Emotionally, it provides a sense of relief: “I’m not weird for thinking this.” Because it’s phrased humorously, it also gives social permission to laugh at oneself, softening the edge of self-criticism. It turns a private discomfort into a socially shared joke, creating unspoken belonging.

Reward II - Self

“This helps me. I’m growing.”
Self Reward is the satisfaction a user feels when they perceive that something they’ve encountered offers personal utility, whether that’s insight, knowledge, perspective, or a tool for coping. Unlike Relatability, which mirrors who the user is, Self Reward points toward who they could become. It activates the aspirational dimension of engagement: the sense that “this content brings me closer to a version of myself I want to reach.” Self Reward is fundamentally about personal growth and empowerment. What differentiates Self Reward from generic “valuable content” is its dependency on modeling: the user must see someone embody the value or behavior in a way that feels believable and adoptable. In SLT terms, observed behavior becomes a catalyst for change only if the model appears to be rewarded for it and the user perceives the behavior as replicable in their own life. If either of those conditions fails (e.g.., the model isn’t actually benefiting, or the behavior seems out of reach) the user’s motivation to imitate will drop (Bandura, 1986). This emphasis on observable reward echoes Bandura’s finding that people are most likely to imitate behaviors that they have seen lead to positive outcomes for others (Bandura, 1977). It also highlights the importance of efficacy: users need to feel “I can do this too.” According to Bandura’s self-efficacy theory, one of the best ways to increase someone’s confidence in doing something is for them to witness a similar other succeeding at it (Bandura, 1997). Thus, effective Self Reward content often showcases a relatable person successfully performing or benefiting from the behavior, providing both inspiration and a sense of attainability. However, not all models function the same way psychologically. According to Self-Categorization Theory (Turner, 1987), people within social groups are most influenced by two types of models: (1) Peers: individuals who reflect the user’s current identity (i.e., relatable people) and (2) Prototypes: in-group leaders or ideal examples. These are figures who are perceived as exemplary representatives of the group’s values or traits. In a digital context, prototypes might be creators, influencers, or community leaders who embody success within a shared identity framework. They may be slightly aspirational, but they remain credible because they represent who the group wants to be, not someone outside of it. These prototypical figures set the standard for what is possible and desirable for group members (Turner et al., 1987). Bandura’s social learning and Turner’s identity theories converge here: users are most influenced when they observe a rewarded identity they can plausibly see themselves growing into.

Behavioral Signature
The signature behavior of Self Reward is the Save. This action implies future applicability, mental rehearsal of behavior adoption, and a belief that this knowledge may be instrumental to personal growth.

How to calculate Self
To understand when Self Reward is truly activated, we benchmark saves against likes, because relatability must precede utility. If a user doesn’t first see themselves in the content (Relatability), they won’t perceive it as useful (Self).
𝑆𝑒𝑙𝑓 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑒 = (𝑆𝑎𝑣𝑒𝑠 / 𝐿𝑖𝑘𝑒𝑠) × 100
This metric identifies how many users moved from emotional recognition to perceived future value. A high Self Rate suggests that content successfully passed the threshold of "this reflects me" into "this can help me." It captures internalized aspiration. The user's sense that they are closer to becoming someone more capable, equipped, or wise.

Actionable Self
SLT tells us that transformation comes from replication. Users are most likely to adopt new behaviors when they see models demonstrating actions that are both believable and replicable. This means content aiming for Self Reward must instruct. The presence of clear, structured, and simple tips allows users to imagine themselves doing the behavior. In contrast, vague encouragement (“be more mindful,” “stay positive”) fails to activate self-efficacy because it lacks observable steps. When content provides actionable guidance (e.g., realistic routines, step-by-step examples, or socially modeled habits) it bridges the gap between insight and adoption. To unlock Self Reward at scale, content must reduce ambiguity and present users with behaviors they can visualize, rehearse, and implement. In practical terms, Self Reward content often answers the question: “I understand the idea, now how do I do it?” By giving that answer in a concrete way, the content puts the users in a position to take the next step.

Example
“You Always Felt Overwhelmed Because You Never Tried These 3 Boundary Shifts”
Why it works: The first half of the title reframes a relatable negative emotional state (overwhelm) as the result of missing knowledge. This is empowering because it creates the possibility that things can change, they just lacked a key behavioral tool. According to Social Learning Theory, transformation happens when users see models demonstrating believable and replicable behaviors. If this post contains simple, well-structured tips like: a script to say “no” to a last-minute meeting, a 2-minute reflective exercise for energy mapping, a phone setting to reduce input overload… then it becomes savable. And that is the key to Self activation. The content avoids generic advice like “prioritize yourself”, which is emotionally valid, but not actionable. Instead, it offers practical tools that users can imagine themselves implementing. The tone is compassionate but directive: “You’ve always felt this way because you didn’t have these tools. Now you do.”

Reward III - Social

“I’m seen. I matter here.”
Social Reward refers to the sense of being acknowledged, appreciated, or validated by others. Unlike Relatability (which is inward-facing) or Self Reward (which is aspirational), Social Reward is fundamentally interpersonal: it satisfies the human need to contribute, be heard, and receive feedback that confirms one’s social presence and relevance. This reward fulfills the relational dimension of identity. Individuals don’t just want to observe or learn; they want to know that their participation makes a difference. Humans have a fundamental need for social acceptance and esteem (Baumeister & Leary, 1995), and Social Reward taps directly into this. Even small nods of acknowledgment serve as powerful psychological rewards because they signal inclusion. According to Leary’s sociometer hypothesis, self-esteem itself can be viewed as an internal gauge of social acceptance; when others approve of or value us, our self-esteem rises (Leary et al., 1995). According to SIT, this kind of recognition plays a key role in self-worth. People derive part of their self-esteem from the status and value they hold within their groups. When users participate in a community and are acknowledged by others, especially by high-status members, they feel an increase in in-group value. In SIT terms, their social identity is being affirmed: the group is effectively saying “you are a valuable member.” Importantly, this validation doesn’t have to be explicit. Even small signals, like a “like” on a comment or an upvote, serve as social markers of inclusion and respect. Over time, these cues accumulate into a sense of reputation or standing within the community. The psychological impact of who validates you also matters. Users are influenced not just by how many people respond, but by who those people are (See the Peers vs. Prototype topic in the chapter before). In community dynamics, a “like” or comment from a respected figure (say, the content creator or a community moderator) can carry disproportionate weight in validating a user’s contribution. It’s essentially an anointing of that user as someone worth noticing, which boosts the recipient’s social standing and, by extension, their self-esteem within the group. From the perspective of SLT, users don’t just observe what content gets rewarded; they observe which forms of participation or contribution (comments, questions, jokes, disclosures) receive social appreciation. This process teaches them how to participate in socially rewarding ways. A user might see that someone who posts a thoughtful comment gets several appreciative replies, and unconsciously learn: “This is how I can be valued too.” Bandura (2001) noted that media environments create rich opportunities for vicarious learning of social norms: people learn appropriate behaviors by witnessing the social feedback others receive. In online communities, this means users are constantly learning the unspoken rules: they calibrate their behavior based on social feedback patterns, developing tacit understandings of what expressions are most likely to be recognized or rewarded. These rules (e.g., “witty comments get likes” or “thoughtful questions get answers”) are informally taught through visibility and imitation. In time, the community’s culture is reinforced as members imitate the behaviors that they have seen earn appreciation. In this way, Social Reward not only gratifies individual users, but also serves as a mechanism of social learning that shapes community norms (Bandura, 2001).

Behavioral Signature
Social Reward is captured through two primary behaviors: Comments, which indicate a shift from internal agreement to public contribution. They signal that a user felt confident enough to speak and invested enough to engage. And Comment likes, which reflect that the user’s contribution was socially appreciated by others, validating both the act and the actor. These are higher-effort behaviors than likes or saves. They involve risk and intent (speaking up, offering an opinion, entering the social arena). Their presence signals that the user feels safe and seen enough to participate.

How to calculate Social
Again, these behaviors usually emerge only after relatability is established. A user won’t comment on content that doesn’t resonate. This is why we frame Social Reward as built on top of Relatability, and why its metric uses likes as the behavioral baseline.
𝐶𝑜𝑚𝑚𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑠 / (𝑆𝑜𝑐𝑖𝑎𝑙 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑒 = 𝐿𝑖𝑘𝑒𝑠) × 100
This metric reflects how many users moved from passive emotional agreement to active, acknowledged participation. A high Social Rate means that the content and the space in which it exists made users feel that their voice was welcome, valued, and rewarded. It is a marker of inclusion

Examples
1 -“@user123, this made me think! Thank you. What do you guys think?”
Why it works: This phrase exemplifies Social Reward by combining vertical validation, peer amplification, and group inclusion into a single interaction. The first part: “@user123, this made me think! thank you” is a powerful gesture of recognition. According to Self-Categorization Theory, when a prototype or high-status figure (like the creator) publicly acknowledges a follower, it boosts the follower’s perceived value within the group. This shifts the user from passive observer to recognized contributor. That feeling of impact is core to social self-esteem. The second part “What do you guys think?” opens the space horizontally. It invites the entire community into the dialogue, implicitly elevating the original commenter by turning their thought into a shared discussion prompt. Other users now observe that insightful contributions get publicly highlighted and extended into conversation. The emotional outcome is twofold: For @user123: validation, pride, and increased willingness to engage. For observers: motivation to contribute in thoughtful ways, now that they’ve seen what social appreciation looks like in action.
2- “Hey guys can you comment with a piece of advice you’d give your younger self? We want to make a list.”
Why it works: The phrase “we want to make a list” gives the comment a sense of contribution to something collective. Users feel their voice matters when their experience is framed as valuable to the group. Reflecting on past struggles and growth also reinforces their identity as someone who’s learned and now belongs. From a Social Learning Theory lens, seeing others share thoughtful responses, and being acknowledged, teaches users that this kind of vulnerability is socially rewarded.

Conclusion

The framework outlined here reveals that meaningful digital engagement is driven by human motivations: the desire to be recognized, to learn, and to connect socially. By aligning content strategy with the three core rewards (Relatability, Self, and Social), teams can design digital experiences that resonate on a deeper psychological level at each meaningful touchpoint they share with users. In essence, content that makes users feel understood, empowered, or connected will inspire more genuine and sustained interaction. By grounding our success metrics in observable user behavior tied to these rewards, we move from vanity metrics to motivational insights. Likes, saves, and comments are not just arbitrary numbers – they are signals of psychological fulfillment. When interpreted through the lens of Social Identity Theory and Social Learning Theory, these behaviors become a map of how people see themselves, what they aspire to, and how they learn to engage with others. In short, our analytics start to reflect human drives rather than just clicks. As digital ecosystems continue to evolve, this framework also serves as an ethical compass. It reminds us that every click, share, or reply is identity in motion. By focusing on rewarding users with belonging, growth, and recognition – rather than just grabbing attention – we create more meaningful interactions. In doing so, we acknowledge the person behind the metric. Ultimately, content that honors these social-behavioral rewards can cultivate not only higher engagement, but healthier communities where users feel seen, capable, and connected.
